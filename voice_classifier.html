Speaker Identification Architecture

Speaker Classification Policy Evolution

V1 — Host-Only Classification

The first system used four binary classifiers trained on ~9,000 clips pulled from 98 episodes using WhisperX and Pyannote. It reached 97.9% validation accuracy, though Chamath and JCal consistently overlapped due to similar cadence and speaking patterns.

The unexpected problem was guests. I had excluded guest audio entirely because guests were irrelevant to my goals, not realizing I needed hard negative examples. Without them, the classifier would happily force unknown speakers into the closest-matching host class even when the probability of a true match was effectively zero. Pretty embarrassing. I took a couple days and made a movie to document it, which was cathartic.

V2 — Human-in-the-Loop Scaling & Embedding Stability

The next iteration introduced Clownfish, a Streamlit-based review system for manually remapping Pyannote speaker IDs into verified speaker labels using synchronized transcript, audio, and video review. Each correction automatically propagated through the transcript and sorted audio into clean per-speaker datasets.

This workflow was enormously effective and allowed me to build the first reliable guest-inclusive corpus. Here is how quickly an episode could be processed:

At the same time, I replaced Pyannote embeddings with Nvidia NeMo / TitaNet, which produced tighter clustering and more stable cosine separation across hundreds of episodes. Episode E136 eventually became the failure case that exposed the system's underlying geometric instability.

Clownfish eventually extracted 85,619 labeled samples across five classes with 97.98% held-out accuracy. More importantly, the embedding space finally behaved consistently enough to trust operationally: the Chamath↔JCal overlap largely disappeared, and boundary failures became interpretable instead of stochastic.

V3 — Structural Failure

The final classifier failed for a more fundamental reason: the label space itself was not cleanly separable.

The system trained only on longer-form audio (>5 seconds). Those segments were classified directly by speaker, and the labels were then propagated back onto shorter clips through shared NeMo cluster IDs.

In episode E136, Brad Gerstner's embeddings overlapped directly with multiple hosts — including JCal, who was not even present in the episode. Some Brad segments sat closer to JCal's voice than JCal's own clips sat to each other, likely because of JCal's unusually dynamic cadence.

Even more concerning, Gerstner was frequently misattributed as Friedberg, who actually was present in the episode. I caught the issue while manually reviewing longer clips that should have been classified directly instead of inheriting propagated labels.

Silent Failure
The system had been failing silently while reporting near-perfect confidence.

Dogfooding the transcripts saved me, and the classifier was removed entirely. I ultimately settled on NeMo clustering plus human-in-the-loop mapping and review, which proved reliable enough that I later expanded the same workflow to catch entirely different classes of transcript corruption discussed elsewhere in the project.
