Project Background

How This Got Built

The goal was a persona-driven chatbot that could reproduce how people speak, not just what they say. The All-In Podcast was the obvious corpus — widely followed in tech circles, hundreds of episodes. The first challenge wasn't modeling language at all. YouTube transcripts lacked speaker attribution, so a custom pipeline was built to convert raw audio into speaker-labeled transcripts — and I got hooked doing it.

Building the pipeline meant needing training data. Getting training data meant building a labeling tool. Having a labeling tool meant actually using it — which meant sitting with thousands of transcript segments, listening, correcting, noticing things. Patterns that a classifier would have scored and moved on from became visible: crosstalk, mic bleed, diarization artifacts, speakers who sounded close enough to each other that no embedding could cleanly separate them. Each layer exposed the one underneath it.

That process produced Clownfish — a Streamlit HITL tool for reviewing and mapping NeMo speaker IDs to named labels at scale. Clownfish evolved into Brody, with integrated heuristics and deeper transcript inspection. From there it became a process of tinkering with audio files and hyperparameters to surface ghost audio — segments where words are heard but never transcribed. The pipeline wasn't a detour. It turned out to be the project, and it's still going.

The chatbot is built. Personas load, inference runs, the voices are recognizable. What remains unsolved is the training — LoRA fine-tuning is on hold while the right data composition gets worked out. The transcripts need to be clean enough, labeled well enough, and representative enough that the fine-tune actually transfers. Which is exactly what all of this has been about.

The pipeline detail is in Voice Classification ↗. The edge cases are in HITL Heuristics ↗. What fell out of all of it is in Chasing Ghosts ↗.
