Motion DNA

For most of the project, video chat was a novelty because it was expensive on my small 12GB Nvidia card, and the Mistral-7B model was limited to relatively small conversational memory.

The recent transition to a distributed Qwen3 architecture on Apple Silicon changed all of that for the better. Not only did I get richer responses, but I recovered most of the GPU memory and gained a dramatically larger context window.

Net-net: video’s back on the menu.

The original MVP host caricatures used South Park-style mouth assets synchronized to speech. It worked, but relied on a growing collection of hard-coded assets and wasn’t particularly friendly to iteration. F5-TTS and VibeVoice both sounded fantastic, but the hosts still felt like robots. Anyone who has watched hundreds of hours of All-In knows the hosts are communicating even when they aren’t speaking.

So I started exploring facial landmark reconstruction, head-pose estimation, body tracking, and other techniques for recovering the non-verbal side of conversation. I’d occasionally cite statistics claiming that most communication is non-verbal to justify the effort, but the truth is simpler: I wanted to make the experience more engaging.

Using facial landmarks, head-pose estimation, and body tracking, the system measures behavioral patterns across hundreds of hours of video. The goal isn’t motion capture or frame-perfect animation. The goal is to recover the tendencies that make each host recognizable.

Runtime Use

not replayed footage — generated behavior
APP Host DNA becomes animation priors
▼
The extracted signals are aggregated into Host DNA profiles and incorporated directly into the runtime. Head-position clusters, gaze behavior, blink patterns, posture shifts, speaking gestures, and conversational reactions become animation priors that drive the avatars during inference. The avatars aren’t replaying recorded video; they’re generating behavior from tendencies learned across the source corpus.

Language captures what the hosts say.
Motion captures how they behave while saying it.
